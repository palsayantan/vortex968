Debouncing 101: Handling Mechanical Noise

In a perfect world, pushing a button creates a clean transition from 0 to 1. In reality, mechanical switches are messy. When the internal metal contacts meet, they physically bounce off each other for several milliseconds before settling into a stable connection. Without debouncing, your microcontroller sees one physical press as a rapid-fire sequence of dozens of presses[cite: 1].

Visualizing the "Bounce"

If you look at a button press on an oscilloscope, you'll see a series of spikes—electrical noise that can last anywhere from 5ms to 20ms depending on the quality and age of the switch[cite: 1].

For a modern processor like an ESP32 or nRF52 running at millions of cycles per second, each of those spikes is interpreted as a separate legitimate logic trigger. This is why a simple "toggle LED" code often results in the LED ending up in a random state or flashing uncontrollably[cite: 1].

Hardware Solution: The RC Filter

The most robust way to fix bounce is at the source. By adding a simple Resistor-Capacitor (RC) network, you create a low-pass filter that "smooths" out the rapid transitions[cite: 1].

As the button is pressed, the capacitor discharges through the resistor at a controlled rate. The mechanical bounces happen too fast to fully charge/discharge the capacitor, resulting in a single, clean voltage slope that the microcontroller can easily interpret. This is especially useful when using hardware interrupts, which are extremely sensitive to noise[cite: 1].

Software Solution: The Timing Method

If you want to save on component costs and PCB space (like in our 25x22 mm designs), you can handle debouncing in firmware[cite: 1].

The logic is simple: when the first transition is detected, the code "ignores" that pin for a short refractory period (usually 20-50ms). After the time has passed, it checks the state again. If the pin is still in the new state, the press is confirmed as valid. While this saves on hardware, it does require careful non-blocking coding using `millis()` to ensure your main loop doesn't freeze during the check[cite: 1].

Engineering Pro Tip: For critical user interfaces, a 100nF capacitor in parallel with your switch and a 10k pull-up resistor is the golden standard for reliable hardware debouncing[cite: 1].

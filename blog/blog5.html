Signal Integrity: Why Trace Impedance Matters

In the world of high-speed digital design, a trace is no longer just a simple connection; it is a transmission line. When signal rise times are faster than the time it takes for the signal to travel across the board, the impedance of that trace becomes the deciding factor between a functional system and a pile of electronic noise.

The Fundamentals of Controlled Impedance

Controlled impedance is the matching of substrate material properties and trace dimensions with locations to ensure that the transmitted signal reaches its destination without reflecting back to the source.

Any change in the cross-sectional geometry of a trace creates an impedance discontinuity. These "bumps" in the signal path cause reflections that distort the waveform, leading to data errors in high-speed protocols like USB 3.0 or PCIe.

Microstrip vs. Stripline Configurations

How you stack your PCB layers determines how electromagnetic fields travel. There are two primary configurations for routing high-speed signals:

  • Microstrip: A trace routed on an outer layer over a reference plane. It is easier to route but more susceptible to EMI and external radiation.
  • Stripline: A trace sandwiched between two ground/power planes. This provides superior shielding and lower EMI but requires more complex PCB stack-ups.
  • Coplanar Waveguide: A trace with ground pours on the same layer, offering excellent isolation for RF signals like those used in nRF52 series SoCs.

The Impact of PCB Stack-up

Impedance is not just about trace width; it is heavily dependent on the distance between the signal layer and its reference plane (dielectric height). A 4-layer stack-up provides consistent dielectric thickness, allowing for repeatable impedance calculations.

When designing for specific impedance (e.g., 50 Ohms for RF or 90 Ohms differential for USB), you must work closely with your fabricator. Variations in Prepreg and Core thickness can shift your impedance by 10% or more, ruining high-speed signal timing.

Design Tip: Avoid 90-degree corners. Use 45-degree bends or rounded corners to maintain a constant trace width through the turn, minimizing impedance changes and signal reflections.

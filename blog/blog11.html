The Essentials of Reverse Polarity Protection

It only takes a fraction of a second to connect a battery or power supply backward. Without protection, this simple mistake can instantly destroy expensive microcontrollers, sensors, and polarized capacitors. Implementing reverse polarity protection is a low-cost insurance policy for any PCB design[cite: 1].

The Series Diode: Simple but Lossy

The most basic method is placing a diode in series with the positive input rail. The diode only allows current to flow when the polarity is correct[cite: 1].

While effective, this method introduces a forward voltage drop. A standard silicon diode drops about 0.7V, while a Schottky diode drops around 0.3V to 0.4V. In 3.3V battery-powered systems, this loss can significantly reduce usable battery life and cause voltage regulators to drop out prematurely. For high-current applications, the diode can also become quite hot[cite: 1].

The PMOS Solution: The Professional Choice

For high-efficiency designs, a P-Channel MOSFET (PMOS) is often the superior choice. By connecting the PMOS in the positive rail with its gate tied to ground, the transistor acts as a switch[cite: 1].

When the battery is connected correctly, the gate-to-source voltage opens the channel, allowing current to flow with extremely low resistance (often just a few milliohms). This results in virtually zero voltage drop and no heat generation. If the polarity is reversed, the MOSFET remains off, effectively disconnecting the rest of your circuit from the power source[cite: 1].

Bridge Rectifiers for Polar-Independent Devices

In some scenarios, you might want your device to work regardless of how the user plugs it in. A bridge rectifier (four diodes) achieves this by routing the current correctly to your circuit every time[cite: 1].

The downside is a double voltage drop (two diodes in series), which makes this unsuitable for low-voltage lithium battery systems but excellent for AC-to-DC conversion or robust industrial inputs where efficiency is less critical than user-friendliness[cite: 1].

Engineering Pro Tip: When using a PMOS for protection, ensure the Gate-to-Source Maximum Voltage ($V_{gs}$) of the MOSFET is higher than your maximum input voltage. For a 6s LiPo system, you may need a Zener diode and a resistor to protect the MOSFET gate from overvoltage[cite: 1].

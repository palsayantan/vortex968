EMI Mitigation in High-Speed PCB Design

Electromagnetic Interference (EMI) is the silent enemy of high-performance electronics. As switching speeds increase and board sizes shrink, managing radiation and susceptibility becomes a mechanical necessity rather than an afterthought. Designing for low EMI ensures that your device not only functions correctly but also passes regulatory certifications like FCC or CE.

The Importance of Return Paths

Every signal is a loop. Current leaves the source and must return to the source. At high frequencies, the return current follows the path of least inductance, which is usually directly underneath the signal trace on the reference plane.

When a return path is interrupted by a gap or a slot in the ground plane, the current is forced to divert around the obstacle, creating a large loop area. Large loops act as efficient antennas, radiating noise across the board.

Differential Pair Routing

High-speed protocols like USB, HDMI, and Ethernet use differential signaling to cancel out EMI. The two traces carry equal and opposite signals, so their magnetic fields effectively cancel each other out.

  • Phase Matching: Ensure the length of both traces in a pair is identical.
  • Constant Impedance: Maintain consistent spacing between traces to avoid impedance discontinuities.
  • Avoid Vias: Minimize the use of vias on differential pairs, as they introduce parasitic inductance.

Effective Decoupling Strategies

Decoupling capacitors act as local reservoirs of charge for Integrated Circuits (ICs). Without them, rapid switching in the IC can cause voltage "sag" on the power rail, leading to high-frequency noise.

For best results, place the smallest capacitor value closest to the IC pin to handle the highest frequencies. Using multiple capacitor values in parallel (e.g., 100nF and 1uF) provides filtering across a wider frequency spectrum.

Stack-up and Shielding

A 4-layer PCB (Signal-GND-PWR-Signal) offers significantly better EMI performance than a 2-layer board. The internal ground plane provides a consistent reference and reduces the distance return currents must travel.

In extremely sensitive designs, such as those involving RF modules like the nRF52 series, consider dedicated metal shielding cans and "via stitching" along the board edges to create a Faraday cage effect.

Pro Tip: Keep high-speed traces away from the edge of the board. Traces near the perimeter can "fringe" their electromagnetic fields into the air, leading to increased radiated emissions.

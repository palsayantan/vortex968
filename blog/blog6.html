Thermal Management in Compact PCB Layouts

As devices shrink, power density rises. In a compact 25x22 mm PCB, heat can become a destructive force, degrading component performance and reducing the lifespan of the device. Managing thermals is not just about cooling—it is about strategic heat transport within the board.

The PCB as a Heat Sink

In small designs, you often don't have room for bulky mechanical heat sinks. Therefore, the PCB substrate and copper layers must act as the primary cooling system.

FR4 is a poor thermal conductor, but copper is excellent. Large copper planes—specifically ground planes—act as a "heat spreader," pulling localized heat away from hot components like power regulators or microcontrollers and distributing it across the entire surface area.

Thermal Vias and Stitching

When a component generates significant heat on the top layer, thermal vias are used to transport that energy to the internal or bottom copper layers.

  • Via Placement: Place vias directly under the thermal pad of the IC (e.g., QFN or DFN packages).[cite: 1]
  • Via Density: An array of smaller vias (e.g., 0.3mm) is often more efficient than a single large via due to increased copper surface area.[cite: 1]
  • Solder Mask: Keep thermal vias open (not covered by solder mask) to allow for better heat dissipation into the air.[cite: 1]

Component Placement for Thermals

Strategic placement can prevent "hot spots" that cause thermal throttling in sensitive SoCs like the ESP32.[cite: 1]

Keep heat-generating components away from temperature-sensitive parts, such as crystal oscillators or high-precision analog-to-digital converters (ADCs). If a power regulator and a microcontroller are both heat sources, separate them as much as the small board dimensions allow.[cite: 1]

Copper Weight and Trace Width

Increasing your copper weight from 1oz to 2oz can significantly improve thermal performance, though it may increase manufacturing costs. For power-heavy traces, wider copper not only reduces resistance-based heating ($I^2R$ losses) but also provides more mass to absorb thermal energy.[cite: 1]

Engineering Insight: Always verify your thermal assumptions with a thermal camera during the prototyping phase. A simulation might say you're safe, but real-world airflow and enclosure constraints can change everything.[cite: 1]
